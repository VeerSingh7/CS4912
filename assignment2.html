Harris Corner Detection & SIFT Feature Matching

Evaluating classical feature detection through Precision-Recall analysis and comparing temporal coherence in SIFT descriptors across consecutive vs random image pairs.

Part A

1. Image & Ground Truth Annotation

A brick bench/wall structure at Ashoka University was chosen for its abundance of mortar T-junctions and structural edges. 60 corners were manually annotated using an interactive plt.ginput tool — clicking directly on brick mortar joints, capstone edges, and structural wall corners.

2. Harris Response Map

The cv2.cornerHarris function was applied with blockSize=2, ksize=3, k=0.04. The response heatmap confirms strong activations at brick mortar joints and structural edges — exactly the regions that were annotated.

3. Thresholded Detection & Precision-Recall

Grid-based NMS (grid=40px) was applied to suppress redundant detections. Using greedy 1-to-1 matching with a 120px distance threshold, the following results were obtained:

Key Observations:
1. Recall decreases monotonically from 100% to 0% as threshold increases — expected since fewer corners survive.
2. Precision remains uniformly low (peak ~1.5%) because Harris detects thousands of real corners (mortar joints, wood grain) that were NOT part of the 60 annotated GT points. These are true corners but count as "false positives".
3. AUC = 0.0108 is low but expected — a denser annotation (500+ corners) would yield significantly higher precision across all thresholds.
4. Best F1 = 0.0288 occurs at threshold 0.10, representing the optimal tradeoff: 1,398 detections capturing 35% of GT corners.
5. At t ≥ 0.30, no GT corners are detected — the strongest Harris responses are concentrated in the foliage/red-wall textures where no GT corners were placed.

Q: Is the total cost lower for consecutive (3) or random pairs (4)? Why?

The total cost for RANDOM pairs (1.35M) is LOWER than consecutive pairs (10.28M).

This is explained entirely by the number of matches, not the match quality. Consecutive pairs produce ~9× more good matches on average (3,891 vs 456) because of high visual overlap between temporally adjacent frames. Since total cost = sum of all individual L2 descriptor distances, more matches naturally produce a higher total sum.

However, the per-match cost reveals the true quality:
• Consecutive: 139.1 avg distance per match (LOWER = better quality matches)
• Random: 155.8 avg distance per match (HIGHER = worse quality matches)

Why consecutive pairs match better:
1. High visual overlap — camera moves slightly between frames, sharing 70–90% of the scene.
2. Similar viewpoint — small angular changes keep SIFT descriptors highly similar, passing Lowe's ratio test easily.
3. Lower descriptor distances — the same bag labels and edges appear from nearly identical angles.

Why random pairs have fewer matches:
1. Random pairs can view opposite sides of the mulch stack (0° vs 180°), sharing almost no common features.
2. Large viewpoint changes cause most descriptors to differ significantly → most fail the ratio test.
3. Only globally distinctive features (Miracle-Gro logo, tree trunk) can survive large view changes.

Notable: The wrap-around pair (oyla_0037 → oyla_0001) has only 51 matches, behaving like a random pair since the camera has orbited nearly full circle. One random pair (oyla_0005, oyla_0007) coincidentally is consecutive and has 6,219 matches — confirming that proximity, not randomness, determines match quality.
